On Stillness

There is a kind of stillness that is not the absence of motion. A river is still when it runs steadily; a house is still when it is fully lived in. The stillness we admire in old rooms is not the stillness of a museum. It is the stillness of long use.

The Table moves all day, and we think it is the stillest object in the room. The bearing traces a path that no one is asked to watch. The pattern completes, and then it un-completes, slowly. It does not need to be looked at to do its work.

We chose motion as the medium because it is the only way an object can show that it is present without demanding to be seen. The Table is on. The Table is drawing. You do not have to attend to it. You can attend to your book.

N° 02 — Process 02 April, 2026 6 min read

The Pattern
and the Room

When a Table is finished, it spends a week alone in the studio. We do not give it instructions during this week. It draws the patterns the studio has taught it, in whatever order it likes, and it listens.

By the end of the week, it knows the sound of our voices, the rhythm of our days, the temperature we keep, the light we use. It knows the small habits we cannot help. It does not learn what we want. It learns what is around it.

This is the only training the Table receives. When it leaves the studio, it does the same listening in your room. After a few months, the patterns it draws are the studio’s patterns, in your accent.

N° 03 — Materials 20 March, 2026 3 min read

A Note on Walnut

We chose European walnut for the frame after a long afternoon spent looking at samples in the studio. It is a wood that holds its silence well: tight grain, warm under the hand, slow to scratch and slow to fade. Oiled, it darkens a little each year. We like that.

Our walnut comes from a single forestry in the Vosges, where the trees are felled in winter and dried for three years before they are sold. We buy from one cutter, who has been doing this for twenty-two years, and who only sells boards he would keep for himself.

The brass is from a foundry near Birmingham. The glass is hand-blown in a small workshop outside Sevilla. The silica is washed by us, in our own sinks, the morning we lay it down.
